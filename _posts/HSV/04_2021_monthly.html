STATEWIDE

  1. To commemorate the 10th anniversary of the April 27, 2011 tornado disasters, Gov. Ivey announced April 27th as a statewide “Day of Remembrance.” Flags were flown at half staff to honor the 254 lives lost and more than 2,200 injured by the deadly storms. Read more.

NORTH AL

  1. Three educators from Limestone County, Tuscaloosa, and Uniontown admitted to reporting false enrollment numbers to receive more state funding. They also admitted to using some of these extra payments for personal use. All three stand to serve up to five years in prison. Their trial begins Sept. 13, 2021. Read more.

MADISON COUNTY

  1. The HSV-Madison County Public Library is planning to launch Blast, a free music streaming service featuring local artists. The library will accept submissions from local musicians until June 30. The streaming platform, which will serve as a documented music collection for the North Alabama area, will debut this summer. Read more.

HUNTSVILLE

  1. The HSV Citizens Advisory Council shared their findings on HSV PD’s response to last summer’s downtown racial protests with HSV City Council. The findings shared that some officers violated department policy in some instances, including inappropriate use of chemical agents, rubber bullets, and bean bag munitions. The report expressed a critical need for more training in response to protests against police violence and racial issues. The 248-page report can be read here, and the recorded proceedings can be watched here.

    More: HSV PD was the only agency that participated with the advisory council’s review, even though multiple agencies, including the Madison PD and Madison County Sheriff’s Office, were present during the protests. No officers were disciplined for their actions during the protest.

    Related: This month, a group trial found two men guilty of disorderly conduct in the downtown protests last summer. Another man was found not guilty, and two dozen others had their charges dropped after agreeing not to sue the city. Five other related cases are still pending. Read more.

  2. SpaceX beat out Blue Origin and Dynetics for NASA’s $2.9 billion contract for the Artemis program. NASA said the decision was based on budget and that SpaceX’s bid was the lowest by far. While this contract covers only the first landing, the next contract competition will concern recurring services to the moon. Read more.

  3. Former HSV Schools superintendent and former Trump assistant Army secretary Casey Wardynski announced he is running for north AL’s Fifth Congressional District. A campaign video posted Thursday describes Wardynski as “Trump’s conservative warrior.” The candidate resigned suddenly from HSV schools in 2016 after disclosing a romantic relationship with the CEO of a private school paid to handle discipline issues for the district. He is the third candidate to announce a campaign for Mo Brooks’ seat. Read more.

MADISON

  1. Madison City Schools have finalized their rezoning plan, including Midtown Elementary School’s opening in the fall of 2021. Rising 5th graders may request to remain at their current school for the next year. Younger students must adhere to the new zoning plan. The plan is awaiting final approval from the MCS Board of Education. Read more.

  2. The Rocket City Trash Pandas President and CEO has resigned and confirmed the organization will not pay the City of Madison the full $1M guarantee. CEO Ralph Nelson says he is resigning to move onto new ventures and that the team lost a projected $17.8M in revenue last year due to the pandemic. The team played no games but laid off no employees; the stadium made $1.79M from 175 other events and $4M through merchandising. Read more.
